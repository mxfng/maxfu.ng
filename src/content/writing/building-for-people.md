I was seven years old when my parents divorced. After their split, I vividly remember having access to a computer at both houses. A friend at school had shown me a brand new game called Rollercoaster Tycoon 2, and I loved it so much that it became a central fixation, an escape from the chaos around me. Going between houses every week, I needed a portable medium to bring my hard earned save files back and forth. I asked for help, and was given a blank floppy disk from my grandpa. Red, matte plastic, with exposed metal components. It slid into the computer and triggered clicks and whirs while my data loaded. Looking back, I might be the last person to have used a floppy disk out of necessity.

The internet used to be fun, whimsical, crazy, and cool, and tech used to feel like a limitless energy, moving and shaking the world around me. Some thirty years ago, I was born in Southern California, on the very edge of the youngest millenials and the oldest Gen Zs, and with that timing came a front row seat to the exponential progress and proliferation of Silicon Valley and the global tech industry. I got to witness brand new concepts, mediums, platforms, and products be born at a rate never before seen in human history, riding the curve of [Moore's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moore%27s_law) while it was still pushing ahead at full force. As a kid, computers and the software within them felt like magic.

## Growing up with technology

I vaguely remember dial up internet. The landline phone plugged into the wall, the grating noises it made while sending requests over the wire, and the mind numbingly long wait times to browse the web. I remember the bubble-shaped monitors with giant housings containing giant tubes, and the headaches their displays caused from all their flickering. Nintendo's Gameboy Advance handheld system was the first computing product that was truly mine, my very own hunk of silicon. It didn't have a backlit screen, so I'd shine a flashlight on it under my bed sheet to continue my Pokemon journey well after bedtime. There's genuine insight in knowing pixel graphics and a 16-bit sound system can create an entire universe in a child's head. We may have over-indexed on graphics in service of the tech while losing grasp on the medium.

I remember the first PC my parents put in my room, a hand-me down. I was still of elementary school age. On it was Microsoft Paint and Windows Movie Maker, two tools that enabled my first significant creative undertaking: Maxinations. I created animated music videos to my favorite songs by drawing each frame in Paint, assembled them piece by piece in Movie Maker, and published them to a brand new platform called YouTube around 2006, garnering thousands of views. I used Microsoft Word and its HTML capabilities to create my own games, using graphics drawn in Paint and invisible, hyperlinked elements for interactivity, around a character named Blobby.

In middle school, I was deeply into filmmaking. I'd received a DigitalBlue camera and was constantly making short videos with friends, self-appointed director of whatever we dreamed up. My dad's best friend wanted to nurture that creativity and gifted me a brand new iMac — my first piece of Apple hardware, and the beginning of a vendor lock-in I'm still struggling with to this day. I'll never forget the excitement of unboxing it, the shock of its design compared to every beige PC I'd used before, and how immediately it became a creative engine. GarageBand for recording songs, Final Cut Pro for editing videos, the Photos app for adjusting images — I was always making something on that machine. Its contents being lost to time is a real personal tragedy.

I remember my first iPhone, the 3G. It came with the amazement of holding such a powerful computer in my hand, learning the nuances of interacting with touch screens, and feeling like this was a big revolution happening. All these things that had been tied down to the desk were now in my pocket. Early social media like Instagram hit the scene, and it was becoming clear that this was the direction that communication was heading. But the backlit screen came around to haunt me. At 13, I had a mental breakdown because my undiagnosed ADHD and its hyperfocus had me skipping sleep for the endless stimulation that was early YouTube, earning me a diagnosis and my phone privileges taken away.

I wasn't a tech kid in the traditional sense. I was a creative kid who got to experience my creativity through technology. Growing up, I created an endless supply of drawings, paintings, songs, writing, comic books, magazines, business ideas, car designs, sci-fi worlds, spinoffs of my favorite games and movies, short films, and brands. The computer was always a means to a creative end that captured my effort through its effectiveness at achieving results. It was a tool and a medium for me to express something that would have been there without it.

## Becoming a programmer

I studied mechanical engineering in college because, embarrassingly, Elon Musk had convinced me that the path to solving humanity's greatest problems was through technology, particularly physical, privately-owned technology. Purely by chance, that somehow led to an internship at Aerojet Rocketdyne doing programming. They needed an intern to translate old measurement device calibration programs, written in BASIC and living on an old HP85 machine, to something more modern, because it was too impractical to keep maintaining a 40-plus year-old computer. Coming up short on mechanical engineering internships and desperate for anything on my decrepit resume, I was willing to lie and say I was the guy for the job.

The project was so open ended, the initial suggestion from my boss was to port it over to an Excel spreadsheet with Visual Basic macros. I opened up Excel, read two lines of Visual Basic and, even with zero programming context, immediately wanted to vomit. There had to be a better way. A few quick Google searches made it clear to me that there was a great first programming language to learn in 2018: Python. Faced with Aerojet's reality of HP85 computers, Excel, and cheap, plastic ThinkPads, there it was: a programming language named after a snake, with an environment called Conda, with an IDE called Spyder. I could hit a play button and watch my work run immediately. How cool, I thought to myself, that tech people got to work with these kinds of products and tools. How cool that you write the code, hit a button, and see the result within seconds. I wanted in. So there I was, my big summer internship in front of me, the project laid out, and Python was my chisel.

I got to work with the metrology engineer on translating his process and equations over to my Python project. It was a pressure transducer calibration workflow. This was a great way to learn Python's syntax by porting over the existing code from BASIC. But it wasn't enough for me to just port it over. It needed a face and a soul. I wanted it to be exceptional. So I brought in the PyQt dependency to spin up a GUI the technicians could use. I worked closely with the technicians to see how they were doing the calibration with the HP85 computer. I learned about GPIB cables and converting sensor outputs to digital data. I named the program REX, after the metrology engineer. I cobbled together a disgusting ball of Python spaghetti around the calibration code to give it life. At the end of the summer, it was built and working, but I hit a wall trying to compile it so the technicians could use it without a Python environment. It stayed on my laptop for another year, but they loved my work so much, they brought me back the next summer to finish it, preserving the old laptop for me. I got two programming internships from my ME degree.

During my second internship, I spent so much time Googling information, syntax, and concepts for Python that something really special happened. The Google Search web page split in half from the center, revealing a virtual terminal environment. Within it, a coding challenge, in Python, with a promise to get a Google interview upon success. I had no idea this was possible. I was terrified. I knew I wasn't a proper software engineer yet, just learning my first language. I stared at it for five minutes, filled with horror and self-doubt, and closed it out without touching it. I deeply regret that choice. There was zero consequence to attempting it. And no matter how much I Googled languages and syntax thereafter, I never saw anything like it again. But the Silicon Valley engineers had shown me just how powerful their platforms were. How creative of them. It only reinforced my desire to be a part of it.

— SECTION TWO NOTES —

This is a founder's story, not an industry critique. The subject is you, not Silicon Valley. The disillusionment is texture, not argument. The essay's arc: wonder → dissonance → a philosophy forged by doing it differently → what you're building now and why.

## The gap (one scene, kept short)

The janitor's interface. This is the single scene that carries the weight of everything wrong. They told you that you were over-indexing on design for the app the janitors use. The managers are the buyers. Display as little as possible to get them to do their job. This pattern repeated: the factory operator at Dirac, the warehouse picker at Flexe. The people whose hands are actually full always get the worst interfaces. The business logic isn't wrong, which is the point. Don't linger here arguing — let the scene speak and move on.

The Flexe version cost you. You built the picker UX when it wasn't the priority and it contributed to the layoff. Building for people has actually cost you something. That matters, and it earns you the right to the philosophy that follows. Name it plainly and don't dramatize it.

One paragraph of compressed texture for the broader industry dissonance — the VC rooms, the decks full of "AI-powered" and "scalable," the gap between what you built and how it was sold. This is atmosphere, not a thesis. Don't give it more than a paragraph. The reader needs to feel the gap, not be lectured about it.

## The through-line (the heart of the section)

You've been doing the same thing your whole life. REX needed a face and a soul — you knew this instinctively before anyone taught you. The technician on the HP85 was obvious to you as a real person. Maxinations, Blobby, the Gameboy under the bedsheet — these weren't tech enthusiasm, they were a creative kid using whatever tool was in front of him. The thread is continuous. The industry kept telling you it was the wrong priority. You kept choosing it anyway.

This is the section where the reader should feel the origin of a design philosophy, not the accumulation of grievances. The tone is closer to conviction than anger. You saw something clearly that the rooms you were in didn't, and you paid a price for it, and you're still here.

## The close

Drumhaus: free, non-extractive, built for anyone who wants to make music. The computer as a medium for something that would have been there without it. This is where the essay lands — not on what's wrong with tech, but on what you're building instead and why it looks the way it does.

The closing paragraph is the design philosophy stated plainly. Keep it. But it should arrive as the natural conclusion of the story, not as a manifesto bolted onto the end. The reader should already believe it before you say it.

## What to cut or compress

- The class argument as a standalone section — fold the sharpest insight (working class people want to do as little as possible on a computer and get back to their lives) into the janitor scene or the closing philosophy. Don't give it its own heading.
- AI as the apex — cut entirely from this essay. You're writing it better and at length in Quarks, AI, and Capital. A passing reference is fine. A full paragraph on how LLMs work belongs in the other piece.
- The romanticization acknowledgment — one sentence at most. "These companies were never innocent, but there was a window where the weirdness was still in charge." Don't elaborate.

## Tone guidance

The failure mode is "angry tech bro mad at tech industry." The success mode is "someone who kept choosing to build for people and is now making that the whole point." Same observations, completely different essay. When in doubt, turn the camera back on yourself — what you built, what it cost, what you learned — not on what the industry got wrong.

deep thesis: "it was a tool and a medium for me to express something that would have been there without it." the whole essay is that sentence.

north star (don't explain this — feel it while writing): the tension isn't socialist vs. capitalist. it's "I see what the industry optimizes for and I think it's leaving money on the table." the janitor's interface isn't charity — it's the insight that the people using the product are the ones who determine whether it actually works. that's a business argument dressed in human language.

I have an almost spiritual reverence for fractals. The core concept: a structure that holds consistently at any given scale, small or large. Look at the universe around you, and you'll see them everywhere. The fractal is like a bridge between the theoretical and the physical. Orderly in its consistency, yet chaotic in its infiniteness. Maybe fractals represent some underlying principle upon which the universe materializes. They show up in the truest empirical sense, and also in the loose, philosophical sense. They're worth sitting with, and pondering deeply.

Physical reality behaves like a fractal. We can reason about its composition at vastly different scales: quarks, atoms, molecules, cells, organisms, ecosystems, planets, stars, galaxies, galaxy clusters, superclusters, the observable universe. But where are the edges of our understanding?

At both extremes, we hit walls. We're able to reason that reality keeps going, but practical limitations of physics restrict us from seeing any further. At the macroscopic scale, the finite speed of light sets a hard boundary. The cosmic background radiation is the oldest light that exists, and beyond it lies the unknown. On the microscopic end, the energy required to see within quarks exceeds our technological capacity.

Physicists tell us the universe is expanding. There may be no hard boundary where it ends, if true. So, on the other end, does it get smaller than quarks?

## Quarks

In the Standard Model of physics, quarks and leptons (like electrons) are treated as point particles. Their behavior can be described irrespective of measurable size or internal structure. But to an observer large enough, a galaxy can be described as a point particle within a supercluster, with similar mathematical accuracy. You yourself could likely describe a river without knowing anything about the molecules inside it. This concept is known as effective theory, or scale independence. The things inside of quarks might simply not matter at the scale we can see. We have theories about what could be smaller: string theory, preon models, the Planck length, none of which I have academic authority to speak to.

Other than the spiritual value of probing the small and understanding the nature of our universe in an abstract sense, are there practical applications to pushing against the edges?

There aren't any right now, and anyone who tells you otherwise would likely be selling something. Existing theories below the quark level are impossible to test, let alone build with. That's not to say that pushing against the edges never got humans anywhere historically: quantum mechanics led to transistors, Einstein's relativity enabled GPS, and particle accelerators gave us the World Wide Web. Fundamental physics operated on a 50-100 year lag before applications emerged, and the future applications were impossible to predict. One might argue that every time humanity has understood a new layer of reality, we've found ways to exploit it.

But what about the spots where that framing hasn't held true? There are entire branches of math -- large cardinal theory, higher-dimensional topology, certain areas of abstract algebra, that have been deeply understood for decades, yet yielded zero practical applications. Most of the low-hanging fruit has already been picked. Electromagnetism gave us the electrical grid, telegraph, radio, and computers. Nuclear physics gave us energy generation, terrifying weapons, and innovations in medicine. Each layer unlocked enormous energy densities and manipulable phenomena for humans to wield. But as we push deeper into physical reality, it takes more energy to access the next layer. We understand the Planck scale, but to test it empirically, we need to produce energies many magnitudes beyond what our most powerful experimental tools provide. Understanding does not guarantee access, and even access doesn't always prove useful.

There's also an element of survivorship bias in the examples I cited. Cherry-picking quantum mechanics and relativity represents the biggest winners in the discipline of physics. There are lesser known works by very smart people that sit in the archives of universities around the world because they turned out to be wrong, hit dead ends, or didn't produce applications. In fact, since the 1970s, particle physics has become a weak case for innovation. The Standard Model has arguably been complete since then. The spinoff technologies from building accelerators (the web, medical imaging advances) came from the engineering challenges, not the physics itself. The strongest examples of exploiting physics for economic gain may come from a specific era, one that may not generalize forward.

Moore's law described how innovations in increased transistor density led to exponential gains in computing performance. That reality drove the computing revolution of the last few decades. Yet today, like other applied sciences that preceded it, it too is coming into contact with its physical barrier: transistor sizes reaching quantum scale. The curve is flattening, and growth is slowing.

Let's put it all in context: every layer of reality we've exploited for growth had a favorable ratio between the energy required to access it and the energy it unlocks, or the control it grants. Chemistry, nuclear fission, and semiconductors all had one thing in common: an extraordinary input-to-leverage ratio at each layer. That ratio might be falling apart for us now, as we push further against the edges of physics. To probe the Planck scale, you'd need a particle accelerator the size of the Milky Way. If you're a realist, that's really disappointing to think about.

Is there a knowledge frontier beyond which understanding is structurally decoupled from application? The honest answer is probably yes. More importantly, we should internalize the idea that our ability to know might eventually permanently outrun our ability to use. The honest case for fundamental research at the deepest scales eventually has to stand on its own terms, rather than hiding behind its potential to unlock exponential growth for industry and capital. That's concerning if you care about science and see how our global economy is structured: if such a wall is being reached, if capital were to turn its back on academia, we might see another dark age upon us.

It was a brief flash in recent human history where scientific fields were unlocked and applications exploded into exponential growth for technology, capital, civilization. We face a reality where many of those fields have long since matured and stabilized. They remain useful, and innovation and progress may be steady, but the stability is now a defining characteristic, and progress becomes incremental. The era for unlocking whole new wings of science and exponential growth in technology, may itself be a historical anomaly.

Where are the current explosive fields of today? Looking forward to the next century, are we hitting the edges for extracting the practical value of understanding reality? The realization that mechanical engineering was a slow-moving, mature field was part of what motivated a younger me to confidently make the jump to specializing in software engineering, where I felt all the excitement and innovation was heading. Even software development has matured and plateaued since its inception.

There may still be candidates, but they are of a different nature from the physics-driven explosions of the 20th century. Biology and genomics may unlock a new wing of science through CRISPR, synthetic biology, and protein folding. Nuclear fusion and quantum computing have captured the imaginations of many smart people, but they're still decades away from practical application, even after an already very long development period. Then there's the elephant in the room: artificial intelligence.

## Artificial Intelligence

Everyone has an opinion on AI, and most aren't well-formed. There are three distinct perspectives, roughly socioeconomic by nature: those of capital, technocrats, and laypeople.

For the capitalist, AI is both a tool and a promise. It's a narrative for labor arbitrage, useful in the here and now as an excuse for layoffs. It also promises a more insidious return, to permanently replace the most expensive input cost: humans. Capitalists play a game of maximizing value extraction at all scales. However, their perspective is internal to the economic engines they command, and limited by it. It's arguably shortsighted of the macroeconomic systems that form the foundation of their revenue: if nobody has a job, who's buying their products and services? But capitalists don't concern themselves with such contradictions. They are motivated by capital alone, depend on a model of permanent growth, and AI provides the steepest curve, and most promising return.

For the technocrat, AI is a path to salvation from what they believe may be a future, permanent underclass. The belief that AGI (artificial general intelligence) is imminent sits at the core of their worldview, and there's a competitive motivation to stay ahead of the curve. They self-select for high intelligence, systems thinking, and contrarianism, and form the builder class: engineers, researchers, founders, VCs adjacent to the technology. For them, technology is the primary driver of human progress, AI is the bleeding edge of present and future growth, and the consequences of its proliferation require competition for survivorship. Their motivations are more personal and diverse than those of the capitalist: salvation for humanity, scientific progress, moral superiority, personal legacy, wealth, and power. In their pursuit, they mistake the steep part of a curve for an infinite trajectory.

For the layperson, AI lurks somewhere between a nuisance and an imminent threat. It's proliferated into all the advertisements they see, the companies they work for, and endless headlines of job cuts. The layperson has the most passive role, and feels powerless to the shifting economic and technological tides. They bear the brunt of the consequences of endless AI expansion and capital consolidation. They're the class of workers, consumers, and citizens. They're fearful of job replacement, and viscerally reject slop in their media, and the environmental costs of training and inference. The capitalists tell them that this is all done in the name of progress; that they must adapt or be left behind. The technocrats attempt to evangelize a similar message: Pandora's box has been opened, and the consequences are wide-reaching and inevitable. Laypeople may lack the framework and specialization to interpret this new technology holistically, but their instincts to fear, scrutinize, assimilate, or reject AI are the most honest of the three perspectives.

None of these perspectives, however, reflect AI accurately. The term is thrown around without precision, to justify cost cutting, sell a path to infinite growth, and galvanize investment. When people talk about AI, they're usually referring to LLMs (large language models). LLMs are the primary AI products on the market, and all the biggest players have their own version.

A large language model predicts what word comes next in a sequence, trained on enormous amounts of human-written text. Through sheer quantity, repeated patterns in how people talk about the world encode deeper structures of reasoning -- structures the model can recombine in ways that feel generative. But it has no persistent memory, no capacity to learn from experience. It references a very sophisticated map of how humans talk about the world, and nothing more.

In 2020, researchers at OpenAI published a paper finding that LLM capability improved predictably as you scaled up in three dimensions: compute used, quantity of training data, and the number of parameters the model had. Parameters are the numerical weights inside the neural network; the values that get adjusted during training to encode what the model learned. This provided ample justification for the arms race that followed: companies could predict how much smarter a model would get by scaling across those three dimensions. Today, they appear to be pushing against increasingly resistant edges. There's a finite limit of human-written data. Even ignoring copyright and intellectual property laws, AI companies have voraciously scraped and ingested the entire internet and some, and all the obvious resources have been pillaged. Synthetic data is one proposed solution, but just like photocopying a photocopy, the practice results in compounding errors over iterations. There is no definitive solution to this problem currently.

Then there's the economics: every AI company lacks a sufficient moat, or specialization that cannot be replicated by competitors. The product every company is offering is near-identical from the user's perspective. Models use the same bland speech patterns, are often sycophantic, and are prone to hallucination. Additionally, commoditization is coming, as price wars suppress revenue and investors expect exponential returns on trillions invested, degrading the quality and affordability of the offering further. Valuations based on shaky AGI assumptions are being undermined by the reality of limitations to scaling, which pose significant risks to investor returns. The narrative of AI is doing more work than the technology itself.

As an early adopter, using LLMs every day to help with software development, communication, planning, business strategy, marketing, scheduling, creative writing, and even sometimes recipes, I've thrown the tool at a wide variety of novel problems, with varying degrees of success. In software, the gains are immediate: it accelerates boilerplate, helps catch bugs, bolsters confidence in pushing into new domains, and provides a conversational interface to unfamiliar codebases and new concepts. For writing, it's a useful sparring partner, good at pushing through slow sections and coaxing out useful ideas via its conversational format. It compresses research time dramatically, and I can make decisions faster because I can externalize my reasoning and pressure-test it in real time. These aspects help me be more productive, and it would be dishonest to not acknowledge them. But the nuance is, LLMs are best characterized when framed as a tool, rather than a new form of sentience. Unsurprisingly, it has limited capability for temporal and spatial problem solving, its usefulness gated by its defining dimension: language.

Attempting to structure a moderately complex schedule falls apart really quickly as updates roll in, overlap, and cancel each other out. Time estimations are random, broad, and generally poorly informed. It loses track of time constantly, reminding me to go to bed in the morning, warning me wrong estimates of how long I've been on a subject or problem. Even simple counting and napkin math can be misconstrued. It has trouble reasoning about physical arrangements, navigation, and geometry. Sending in measurements of things yields low-quality responses. It's generally too sycophantic to provide real actionable social advice, and is almost always biased to my perspective.

The hallucination can be egregious, especially paired with the confidence of its responses. I once sent a typo while asking a question about Los Angeles' Sawtelle neighborhood, mislabeling it "SoTel", and it confidently responded with an entirely made-up neighborhood story, complete with an early Jewish immigrant backstory, street-car era architecture, a famous Asian-American food scene, and real streets as boundaries.

LLMs also suffer from a lack of taste. They prefer the average, the bland, and the mundane. Their choices and opinions are just a weighted consensus biased by training data and user input. Humans form their creative base by ingesting novel and interesting materials they curate by spending intentional time with them. When you're trained on everything, you're biased towards the center. This means that an LLM's work might be competent, often very good depending on the use case, but structurally incapable of being genuinely unique or opinionated. Taste is a strong, coherent, unjustifiable preference that defies averages and sometimes turns out to be the correct position despite them. It's what makes creative work compelling and attractive. An artist has to be willing to be wrong in a specific direction, and take creative risks. LLMs are very bad at that.

There is also a pattern of laziness in LLMs probing for information, or defending their arguments. They're most likely to take a user input at face value and choose the path least resistance, rather than scrutinize or dissect. Even with all the tools they have available, like searching the web, or reading referenced documents, they will only do so if prompted. When reading code, they might look at one file and then assume the functionality of methods imported from another, and make a false assessment of the design. But laziness is anthropomorphizing a more unsettling philosophical reality of their work: they have no stake in being right. They have no professional reputation, no memory of past failures, and no consequences for being wrong. When challenged, they simply flip their position whenever it's most convenient. They have no accountability at any level.

This is where the capitalists and technocrats get LLMs wrong: they cannot replace a human being, no matter how adept they become at solving problems across domains, because the replacement can never be one-to-one. You aren't replacing labor with cheaper labor, you're replacing labor with something categorically different that still requires human labor for every decision that matters. The speculative cost savings evaporate under the need for oversight on every consequential decision. There is no continuity of consequence for an LLM.

But even setting aside these practical failures, there's a deeper structural issue. Unlike quarks, artificial intelligence as a field isn't really uncovering a new layer of reality. It isn't giving humans access to a previously inaccessible physical phenomenon with favorable energy-leverage ratio. It's an applied science, one composed of very mature fields: linear algebra, statistics, and compute. It's a technology that reorganizes existing human-generated information more efficiently. AI serves as a logistics optimization on top of information, making existing human knowledge more accessible and recombinant, which is genuinely useful, but a different category from the explosions that preceded it. The gains are real, but bounded.

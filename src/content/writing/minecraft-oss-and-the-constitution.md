Recently I've been playing a lot more Minecraft than I'd like to admit. It's always been a guilty pleasure of mine; a safe place to disconnect from the scary real world and reconnect with my inner child and builder. There's a popular server called EarthMC where I've been spending some time recently. The premise is interesting: create a scaled-down Minecraft reconstruction of the Earth's topography, throw in some mods that allow players to claim land, hinged on an economic system of trading gold for resources, and let them run wild in Minecraft's mechanics and gameplay loop. There's markets, stores, towns, nations, alliances, treaties, hunters, wars, and all different sorts of geopolitics and role playing.

One thing I noticed quite quickly is that the system is neutral to presence, or time played on the server, but quite punishing towards absence. If you're a mayor of a town, and you're offline for more than 42 days, you're replaced by someone next in line. You can't log in to gather resources and restock your shop? Players start spawning somewhere else. A lot of shops rely on word of mouth to keep players coming in. It's a never-ending treadmill that rewards players who don't have much else going on in their lives, which is unfortunate. A lot of the coolest projects are run by one or a few players, totally reliant on their willpower and vision to be seen through. When they're offline, nothing gets done at all. And that's a shame, because there's constantly 500 or more people online, playing at any given time. Many drop in throughout the day, yet their time and play doesn't seem to build constructively towards a shared civic goal, even within towns.

What hooked me into this system, and I say system deliberately here, reveals a through-line in other facets of my life and interests: I'm particularly fascinated by the design of systems that produce reliable output from unreliable participation. Systems whose correctness does not depend on any particular human being good, present, or motivated. It's very specific, but very interesting to reason about. I spent a lot of my time at Dirac, when I proposed and led a rebuild of their flagship software product, BuildOS, thinking about this problem. How could choices in developer tooling, CI, test coverage, and documentation make pain points like onboarding, maintenance, and feature additions as painless and friction-free as humanly possible?

That kind of problem echoes what I assume the American founders were chewing on when they drafted the Constitution, what Linus Torvalds had to evolve the Linux project into over decades, and in the most generalizable sense, what every human-made system has to endure to survive, virtual or not. You see, a town on EarthMC is a lot like a little government, or open source software. The creator of a town or open source software should ask themselves: if any one person leaves, how is this system modular, flexible, and well-architected enough to evolve to meet future demands, funnel in new contributors, and in the case of EarthMC, keep players having fun?

If 95% of contributors are drive-by strangers, you cannot select your users, cannot compel them, cannot predict them, and the thing has to work anyway, what generalizable principle, if any, survives?

The system must assume its contributors are anonymous, transient, and unmotivated, and still produce cumulative output. Not just "resilient to a maintainer leaving." Resilient to never having a reliable maintainer in the first place.

Good architecture lets a competent person understand the system fast. In the EarthMC regime, the uncommitted stranger with no context should ideally be able to contribute correctly without understanding the system at all, and makes it hard to do wrong. The task surface should be designed so that the right action is the path of least resistance: guardrails, defined zones, specs that state not just what, but exactly where, and how. Idiot-tolerant by construction is of a much higher design standard than building something only experts can extend. There's beauty in that line of problem solving.

Mature OSS projects survive on overwhelmingly drive-by contributors. The whole discipline is mechanisms for converting unreliable, unaligned, transient labor into cumulative output, which I've listed here side-by-side with potential EarthMC equivalents:

- Good first-issue labels; a public build queue
- Contribution guidelines and templates; a spec format
- Continuous integration that rejects bad input before a human sees it; role-permissioned zones where a stranger can't break the design
- Maintainers who review and merge rather than write everything; the author as bottleneck-remover, not bottleneck

But you can't crowdsource taste or vision from strangers. At least, not reliably. The real fix for any resilient project with these characteristics is two separate layers:

1. The visionaries: a few people who hold the vision and write specs

2. The labor system: a well-designed funneling mechanism that galvanizes anyone to execute the time-consuming portion of it

What I'm trying to acknowledge is that it's okay to have a little centralization in human systems. Holding the vision for a project and executing the bulk of it are two different kinds of work, and a system runs better when it doesn't force everyone to do both. Some people, on a given project, want to shape its design, aesthetics, and core purpose. Others would rather drill into the details, grind along on a well-defined task, or support rather than lead. A good system capitalizes on that by letting each kind of work find the person who wants to do it that day, not by sorting people into a class of thinkers and a class of doers. When I volunteered at Our Big Kitchen LA, I was part of a conveyor belt of people hand-rolling hundreds of meatballs to help feed hungry people. The layer you occupy is a role, not a rank. It's contextual, and it moves. I can be a meatball roller and a product's key architect in one lifetime, even within a single week.

I don't know exactly how to construct a town, or nation for that matter, in EarthMC, that survives the glaring failure mode of its visionaries and most active players churning. I also don't know what perfect system could capture all the strangers rolling through to contribute their time effectively to a grander effort. I'd assume the average player logs on, gets recruited into a town, then plays for an hour or so before never logging on again. But to make every minute count, while still acknowledging that reality, that kind of solution would be worth the payoff. If a big megaproject were completed by hundreds of scattered contributors, that's not so different from open source software. It's an outcome worth pursuing as an ideal.

I also admit that I'm not the most qualified to construct a software system that upholds the high standard I've defined here either. I've at least had experience making solid guesses as to what that solution shape looks like in practice, and I can reasonably say I achieved something close to the ideal in watching Dirac's continued success after my departure, but I can't say for certain. Building out good project structure and code structure, hand-selecting the right dependencies, automating standards as much as possible through unit tests, linting, formatting, end to end tests, and writing solid documentation where practical is definitely a good start.

A powerful tool to leverage is "monkey see, monkey do" in humans. Lay the right foundation, make the patterns obvious enough, and most reasonable people will walk in, see the writing on the wall, and extend the pattern, sometimes subconsciously. This is as true in software as it is in broader memetic culture, and probably true in Minecraft as well. There's a weird order to how the average person can integrate their work into a larger pool of labor seamlessly, across domains. I guess people just naturally copy stuff they see and think is good, and in a project, they'll follow the shape of the existing groundwork. Make sure the foundations you lay are indicative of a cohesive and replicable execution path where possible. For any big project, the beginning might be the most important time for design and taste.

The coolest validation of that was when I set up a state management system for part visibility for our CAD file viewer. I'd routed it through the React UI for the editor component, and wired everything up in a dedicated Redux slice. All the business logic for how visibility interacted with the hierarchical part tree was covered. However, it wasn't connected to the 3D viewer. I'd built a black box for managing the state of what was visible and what was hidden. This covered the part tree and the application business logic, but it didn't actually show or hide the parts yet.

Another engineer, who joined after me, was given the task of connecting the state machine to the actual CAD viewer, a separate area of code built on a complex third-party dependency. This was his first big assignment at the company. That engineer was given little to no information from my end about my API or the technical details, only the feature's requirements. He successfully connected the two pieces without any help from me. I was only a little upset when he was praised for the full visibility feature as if he'd built it end-to-end, when in reality I'd solved half the problem for him. Semantics though, really, the payoff of watching good design prove modular for follow-on execution is the kind of story that keeps me going when the job and the industry get tough.

What was I talking about anyway? Oh right, Minecraft, OSS, and the Constitution. For your next project, side hustle, company, Minecraft town, co-op, collective, nonprofit, whatever it is, aim to build something resilient to whatever random person the universe throws at maintaining it. Designing is only part of the battle. Willing the idea into existence is another. But the most important part is the vast, often untapped resource of every other human out there who might love adding to and maintaining what you started. Design for them from the start, so you don't regret it later. Happy building.

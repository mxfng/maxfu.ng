This is all fast and loose...

Restrictive single family zoning and LA's housing crisis means old postwar working-class housing is swapped for box mansions for top-1% enclave, without any additional capacity added to serve the housing crisis. This is potentially a good analogy for the concentration of wealth that is happening pervasively across American society, represented in our built environment.

The teardown-and-rebuild cycle in Mar Vista is wealth concentration expressing itself in physical space.

A neighborhood built explicitly as affordable postwar working-class housing has been systematically converted into a top-1% enclave, one box mansion at a time.

The Venice Boulevard zoning line that divides the neighborhood encodes the entire political economy of LA housing in a single street.

Housing's dual function -- as asset and as shelter -- is fundamentally in conflict.

Homes have appreciated $170K in a single year. The old community is quietly displaced with each new construction. And there is a lot of construction.

Venice Blvd is a hard zoning boundary: apartments and multi-family to the south; SFR exclusivity to the north. It's been that way for a very long time. The street represents the entire ideology of LA housing policy and it's very visible to me as I walk the neighborhood everyday.

I take leisure walks to the north of Venice blvd, admiring the homes. I also often venture south across the boulevard a few times a week as I go to the gym down on Washington.

1. Mar Vista was built to be affordable — that's the irony

The neighborhood north of Venice Blvd was filled out in the 1950s–70s with modest postwar SFR homes — originally lower-cost options on what was then the city's fringe. They were built as basic, low-cost homes. As the Westside became a major business and tech hub, those same homes now sit on land worth $1M+ before a shovel touches the ground. The premise of the neighborhood has been inverted.

1. The teardown math — why boxes, not palaces

When land costs $1.5M+, a developer must build the most expensive thing the zoning allows to justify the acquisition. SFR zoning means one unit, so you build the largest, most expensive single house possible. This produces the box. There's no incentive for craft or ornament — design quality is overhead, not value. The money goes to land and square footage, not architecture.

Contrast: Hancock Park was built when land was cheap and craft labor was abundant. The money went into the building. The architectural regression tracks the inversion of land/labor cost ratios.

1. Who's actually buying — the buyer profile breakdown

DINK tech households — two senior tech workers ($275K each). Individual income isn't shocking; the pairing creates the purchasing power. Silicon Beach employment cluster (Google, Snap, Apple TV+, Amazon, HBO) within 10 min.
Equity-out buyers — someone who sold in Seattle/Austin/Bay Area with $800K in equity arrives with a transformed down payment. Not earning their way in — laundering prior market gains.
RSU/IPO liquidity events — mid-level engineers who vested $1-2M in stock pre-2020. One-time event, not ongoing income. Gets them in the door despite payment math.
Parental wealth transfer — underreported. The $400K down payment increasingly comes from a parent's home equity in a prior generation's cheaper market.
Key point: these buyers aren't earning their way to $2M. They've accumulated capital through equity, inheritance, or optimized partnership. It's less about income, more about which cohort got liquid at the right time.

1. The doom loop — why it accelerates

Each new $3M box raises the comparables for every adjacent parcel. The neighborhood reprices around the new construction, which then attracts the next developer. SFR zoning prevents the only thing that would slow it — spreading land cost across multiple units. Upzoning would actually reduce luxury teardown pressure, but homeowner-voters block it. The policy that's supposed to protect the neighborhood is the mechanism of its transformation.

1. Hancock Park contrast — the HPOZ as preservation/exclusion mechanism

Hancock Park's Historic Preservation Overlay Zone legally prohibits what's happening in Mar Vista — teardowns and box replacements are simply off the table. This preserved the architecture. But the economic effect is a permanent upper ceiling on the buyer pool: you can only buy the full historic home at full price. The same tool that saved the Tudor Revivals also guaranteed the neighborhood remains accessible only to inherited or highly concentrated wealth — forever. HPOZs are framed as community preservation but function as exclusionary zoning with better PR.

"Hancock Park's illiquidity isn't just old money and Prop 13 — it's that even if a developer bought in, the HPOZ means they legally can't do what they're doing in Mar Vista."

1. The architectural decay argument

The aesthetic quality of wealth expression has declined even as prices have risen. Three interlocking causes:

Economics: craft labor (plasters, stonemasons, ornamental ironworkers) barely exists at scale in the US anymore. Even a willing developer can't easily source it.
Ideology: Modernism declared ornament dishonest and regressive (Loos, "Ornament and Crime," 1913). Architecture schools propagated this for 70 years. The box isn't just cheaper — it's the physical expression of a century-long cultural argument.
Asset logic: When housing is purely an investment vehicle, design is overhead. Ornament doesn't add to the appraisal.
